Azerbaijan, landlocked country, occupying an area that fringes the southern flanks of the Caucasus Mountains. Azerbaijan is bounded on the north by Russia, on the east by the Caspian Sea, on the south by Iran, on the west by Armenia, and on the northwest by Georgia. The exclave of Nakhichevan is located southwest of Azerbaijan proper, bounded by Armenia, Iran, and Turkey. The capital of Azerbaijan is the ancient city of Baku, whose harbour is the best on the Caspian Sea. It is an internationally accepted and acknowledged reality that Azerbaijan, located in a geographical space where different religions and ethnic cultures meet, has a rich cultural and spiritual heritage and traditions of multiculturalism and tolerance. The dense river network of the republic covered its territory with a blue spider's web. Kura and Araz rivers are the largest rivers of the Caucasus and are the country's main sources of irrigation and hydroelectric power. Also, Azerbaijan is famous for its oil and gas fields. 2/3 of the republic's territory is rich in oil and gas. A number of diseases are treated on the basis of Naftalan oil, which is famous in the world. The republic also has mineral water reserves with different chemical composition which differs in their therapeutic value. Due to its richness, the territory of Azerbaijan is called "museum of mineral waters". Then, there are a lot of thermal springs in the Talysh mountains.
Azerbaijanis have a rich and distinctive culture, a major part of which is decorative and applied art. This art form is represented by a wide range of handicrafts, such as chasing, jeweling, engraving in metal, carving in wood, stone, bone, carpet-making, lasing, pattern weaving and printing, and knitting and embroidery. In November 2010 the Azerbaijani carpet was proclaimed a Masterpiece of Intangible Heritage by UNESCO. Azerbaijani architecture typically combines elements of East and West. Many ancient architectural treasures such as the Maiden Tower and Palace of the Shirvanshahs in the Walled City of Baku survive in modern Azerbaijan. Music of Azerbaijan builds on folk traditions that reach back nearly a thousand years. Among national musical instruments there are 14 string instruments, eight percussion instruments and six wind instruments. Mugham is usually a suite with poetry and instrumental interludes. When performing mugham, the singers have to transform their emotions into singing and music. In contrast to the mugham traditions of Central Asian countries, Azerbaijani mugham is more free-form and less rigid; it is often compared to the improvised field of jazz. UNESCO proclaimed the Azerbaijani mugham tradition a Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity on 7 November 2003.
